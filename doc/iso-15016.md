# ISO 15016 — Framework Explainer

*Context: Speed Loss Dashboard — the reference speed–power curve and the sea-trial analysis that
produce it are what ISO 19030 measures speed loss *against*. ISO 15016 is the standard for that
upstream stage: turning raw runs into a corrected, still-water speed–power curve.*

> **This document explains the standard. It does not describe what this codebase does.**
> For that — the declared deviations, the known gaps, and what a number from this dashboard
> may and may not be used for — read
> **[`iso-19030-conformance.md`](./iso-19030-conformance.md)** (deviation 2, and the
> reference-curve gap). In short: the ISO 15016 wind/wave correction here is **computed,
> scored, and rejected** (the no-correction control wins), so `power_corrected_kw` is measured
> power **passed through unchanged**; and the reference curve is **fitted from in-service noon
> reports, not sea trials**.

---

## What ISO 15016 Actually Is (Scoping First)

Full title: **Ships and marine technology — Guidelines for the assessment of speed and power
performance by analysis of speed trial data** (2015, revising the 2002 edition).

One relationship defines its place:

- It sits **upstream of ISO 19030**. ISO 19030 measures *change* in performance against a
  baseline curve, and its uncertainty budget explicitly **assumes that curve was corrected per
  ISO 15016**. ISO 15016 is the standard that *makes* the baseline; ISO 19030 is the standard
  that *watches it drift*.
- Its scope is a single transformation: **raw sea-trial runs → the contract-condition,
  still-water speed–power curve**. It takes runs measured on a real day — with current, wind,
  waves, a particular water temperature, a particular displacement and trim — and removes every
  one of those environmental effects to recover the curve the ship *would* have made in flat
  calm at the contract loading.

The 2015 revision tightened the wave and current methods over 2002. The same corrected curve
also underpins **EEDI verification** at sea trial, which is why the standard is written to a
contractual, not merely diagnostic, level of rigour.

---

## The Core Quantity: The Corrected Curve

The output is a **corrected speed–power curve** `P_corr(V)` — the shaft power required at each
speed **at contract displacement and trim, in still water, deep water, no wind, no waves**.

Distinguish two things that are easy to conflate:

- **Measured-trial power** `P_meas(V)` — what the engine actually delivered on the trial day,
  contaminated by everything the environment added.
- **Corrected power** `P_corr(V)` — `P_meas` with every environmental added-resistance term
  subtracted and every non-contract condition normalised away.

`P_corr(V)` is exactly the curve ISO 19030 later inverts to ask "at this power, what speed
*should* a clean hull make?" — the `V_expected` in the speed-loss formula.

---

## The Correction Chain

ISO 15016 is, in essence, an ordered subtraction. Each step removes one thing the real day added
(the analogue of ISO 19030-2's processing chain):

| # | Step | What it removes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Trial conduct** | Double / reciprocal runs on opposing headings, at steady state, in deep water, inside a weather window — so current and transients are *symmetric* and thus removable. |
| 2 | **Current** | The **'mean of means'** iterative method over the reciprocal-run pairs, which cancels a current that is constant over the run pair. |
| 3 | **Wind** | Added resistance from *relative* wind, via a wind-resistance-coefficient dataset (e.g. **Blendermann**) resolved onto the bow. |
| 4 | **Waves** | Added resistance in waves — **STAWAVE-1** (empirical, head-sea) or **STAWAVE-2** (spectral, directional), depending on data available. |
| 5 | **Water temp & density** | Normalise resistance to the standard seawater temperature and density (viscous resistance scales with both). |
| 6 | **Shallow water** | The **Lackenby** correction, where trial water depth inflates resistance. |
| 7 | **Displacement / trim** | Normalise the run's actual loading to the **contract** displacement and trim. |
| 8 | **Power / RPM → fit** | Reduce the corrected runs to a curve — the still-water `P_corr(V)`. |

For the **exact coefficients, the mean-of-means iteration, and the wave spectra**, go straight
to the standard text. The formulae in `physics.py` are a defensible subset (see below); hard-
coding the full ISO 15016 coefficient tables from memory would be worse than not implementing
them.

## Mapping Back to Your Dashboard

**What is implemented.** `ym_datalake/etl/physics.py` re-derives two of the correction chain's
resistance terms:

- **Wind** — Blendermann longitudinal drag (`wind_resistance_n`), single head-on coefficient
  `C_AA ≈ 0.85`, cosine angular shape (following wind thrusts, so the term can be negative).
- **Waves** — STAWAVE-1 (`wave_resistance_n`), `R_AWL = (1/16)·ρ·g·Hs²·B·√(B/L)`, with a
  head-sea taper (full within 45° of the bow, zero abeam).
- Both are turned into a power correction by `resistance_to_power_kw` at a quasi-propulsive
  efficiency **η = 0.70**.

`ym_datalake/etl/curated/corrections.py` then wires these into `correct_row`, and — critically —
into `choose_convention` / `detrended_scatter`, which **score** the correction rather than
assume it helps.

**Applied to the wrong runs, and rejected.** Two things separate this from a textbook ISO 15016
application:

1. It runs on **daily noon reports, not trial runs**. There are no double/reciprocal runs, no
   steady-state deep-water window — just one 24-hour mean per ship per day.
2. The correction is **computed → scored → rejected**. `detrended_scatter` measures each arm's
   own residual error (speed-loss spread after the fouling trend it is *meant* to measure is
   removed). The no-correction control wins:

   | convention | detrended speed-loss sd |
   |---|---|
   | **none (control)** | **4.432 pp** |
   | bow_relative | 4.878 pp |
   | true_compass | 4.940 pp |

   So `convention='none'` is selected, and **`power_corrected_kw` is `horse_power` passed
   through unchanged** — the column name is a retained misnomer, and `correction_applied` /
   `correction_convention` land the truth on every row. The resistances are still computed and
   exposed (`resistance_wind_kn` / `resistance_wave_kn`) for the weather cost-attribution
   channel; they are simply never allowed near the speed loss. **Weather is handled by ISO
   19030's Beaufort ≤ 4 filter gate, not by a correction term.**

**Which ISO 15016 steps are absent.** Read against the chain above:

- **No current correction** (step 2) — the source already carries speed *through water*, and a
  noon report has no reciprocal runs to average a current out of.
- **No water-temp/density normalisation** (step 5).
- **No shallow-water resistance correction** (step 6) — shallow water is handled as a *filter
  gate* (`deep_water_min_m`), i.e. rejected, not corrected.
- **No trim correction** (step 7) — the reference curve is **displacement-only**, so there is no
  trim reference to normalise to (see `iso-19030-conformance.md`, "No trim filter").

**The reference curve itself.** `ym_datalake/etl/raw/reference_curve.py` fits
`P = a·V^n·(Δ/Δ_ref)^(2/3)` from **in-service clean-window noon reports, not sea trials**. This
is a **known gap**, not a deviation: ISO 19030 expects a curve corrected per ISO 15016 (or model
tests / CFD), and this curve inherits in-service scatter instead. Its inverse — `clean_speed_kn`
(`V_expected`) — is the ISO 15016 → ISO 19030 hinge, consumed by
`ym_datalake/etl/curated/daily.py` to produce `speed_loss_pct`.

## Uncertainties You Must Confront Honestly

- **The direction convention is unrecoverable.** `WIND_DIRECTION` is a 16-point compass index the
  dataset README calls "相對/羅經 (relative/compass)" — genuinely ambiguous — and there is **no
  heading column** to disambiguate it. `true_compass` routes the angle through a *fabricated*
  heading yet scores within 1.3 % of `bow_relative`, which means the column carries **essentially
  no information** for the correction under either reading. Multiplied by an **estimated** windage
  area (`transverse_area_m2` is flagged not-derivable), at a Beaufort ≤ 4 gate where the true
  wind effect is already small against the ~15 % power-channel scatter, the correction adds
  **~0.5 pp of noise and removes nothing** — which is why it loses to the control.
- **No sea trials means the curve inherits in-service scatter.** Fitted on noon reports, the
  curve carries a **median intercept** (so ~half of clean-hull days sit at a *negative* speed
  loss by construction) and **pooled offsets** for the four ships below the per-ship fit floor
  (**S21, S22, S8, S6** — their trends hold, their absolute levels do not).
- **Coarse coefficients.** A single Blendermann `C_AA` for all headings and loadings; STAWAVE-1's
  head-sea taper only (no true directional spectrum). These are honest first-order physics, not
  the full ISO 15016 coefficient tables.

## Architecture / Handoff Note

ISO 15016 is the **upstream baseline stage** of the pipeline — everything ISO 19030 does is
relative to its output. Three things to keep true:

- **Keep the resistances computed and exposed even when the correction is rejected.** They are
  real physical estimates and the weather cost-attribution channel depends on them; rejection is
  a decision about the *speed loss*, not about whether the physics is worth having.
- **Version the convention per row.** `correction_applied` / `correction_convention` are landed on
  every `fact_performance_daily` row so no downstream consumer has to infer "corrected?" from a
  misleading column name — and so re-scoring on new data can change the answer without rewriting
  history.
- **The ISO 15016 → ISO 19030 hinge is one function**: `V_expected = clean_speed_kn`, the inverse
  reference curve. Everything about baseline quality — sea-trial vs in-service, corrected vs raw,
  pooled vs per-ship — enters the whole system through that single point.
